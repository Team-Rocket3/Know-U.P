Folk and Performing Arts

Uttar Pradesh boasts a rich and vibrant tradition of folk and classical performing arts that reflect the cultural, religious, and social fabric of the region. These art forms are deeply rooted in the everyday lives of the people and are performed on various occasions such as festivals, seasons, religious events, and social gatherings.

Music

Hindustani Classical Music: Uttar Pradesh has been a cradle for Hindustani classical music, giving rise to prominent gharanas (musical lineages), each with its distinctive style, vocal techniques, and repertoire.

Varanasi Gharana: Known for its emotive and devotional quality, especially in Dhrupad and Khayal singing. Great musicians like Pandit Bismillah Khan (shehnai maestro) hailed from this region.

Rampur Gharana: Originated in the princely state of Rampur; it preserved the older traditions of Dhrupad while incorporating Khayal. Vocalists here emphasize clarity of notes and intricate taans.

Lucknow Gharana: This gharana is better known for its tabla and Kathak traditions, but it also contributed significantly to vocal music, especially thumri and dadra, which are semi-classical forms rich in emotional expression.

Folk Music Styles:

Birha:Originating from eastern UP, Birha is a powerful form of folk singing that reflects the struggles and emotional experiences of migrant laborers. The songs express separation, longing, and the hardships of rural life.

Kajri:Sung during the monsoon season, especially by women, Kajri captures the beauty of rains and the pain of separation from loved ones. It is often performed during Sawan (rainy season) festivals.

Chaiti:Sung in the month of Chaitra (March-April), these songs are devotional in nature and often dedicated to Lord Rama or Shiva. The themes revolve around love, devotion, and seasonal transitions.

Alha: A martial ballad sung in a heroic tone, recounting the bravery of the legendary warriors Alha and Udal. These performances are loud, energetic, and often accompanied by traditional instruments like nagada and dholak.

Rasiya: Predominantly popular in the Braj region, Rasiya songs are romantic and centered on the love of Radha and Krishna. These are usually sung during Holi and other Krishna-related festivities.

Dance

Kathak:

  • Kathak, one of the eight classical dance forms of India, has its roots in northern India, especially in Lucknow and Varanasi.
  • It evolved from storytelling traditions (katha) by wandering bards, and later flourished in Mughal courts, which influenced its intricate footwork, graceful movements, and expressive facial gestures.
  • The Lucknow Gharana of Kathak is renowned for its subtle gestures, delicate expressions, and strong emphasis on abhinaya (expression).
  • Themes often include mythological stories, especially of Krishna and Radha, as well as Persian-influenced court dances.

Folk Dances:

Raslila:

  • A theatrical dance performance based on the life of Lord Krishna, especially his childhood and youthful interactions with Radha and the Gopis (milkmaids).
  • Performed predominantly in the Braj region (Mathura-Vrindavan), Raslila combines narration, dance, music, and acting, often staged during Janmashtami and Holi.
  • It is a devotional performance and is considered a way to witness and participate in divine play (lila).

Nautanki:

  • A folk theatre form combining song, dance, drama, and humor. It is highly entertaining and has historically served as a medium for social commentary and moral lessons.
  • Popular in rural and semi-urban areas, Nautanki is performed on mobile stages, with live music and dramatic storytelling.
  • Themes can range from mythological tales to contemporary social issues, making it a dynamic art form that evolves with time.
