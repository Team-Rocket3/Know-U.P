Art and craft

Uttar Pradesh is a vibrant hub of traditional arts and crafts, many of which have roots in centuries-old practices that continue to thrive today. These crafts are not only a source of livelihood but also embody the region’s rich cultural and aesthetic heritage.

Chikankari – The Elegant Embroidery of Lucknow

Chikankari:Chikankari is a delicate and intricate form of hand embroidery that originated in Lucknow, often referred to as the "City of Nawabs." This art dates back to the Mughal era, believed to have been introduced by Empress Noor Jahan. Traditionally done on fine fabrics like muslin, cotton, and chiffon, Chikankari involves white thread embroidery that creates floral patterns and motifs. Today, it is used in sarees, kurtas, dupattas, and even home décor items. It is especially known for its understated elegance and has garnered global appeal.

Zardozi – The Regal Gold and Silver Embroidery

Zardozi:Zardozi is a form of opulent embroidery that uses gold and silver threads, along with pearls, sequins, and precious stones. Once patronized by the Mughals and used to embellish royal attire, tapestries, and ceremonial accessories, Zardozi remains a symbol of luxury. Artisans from Lucknow, Bareilly, and Varanasi are particularly renowned for their skill in Zardozi work. Today, it is commonly seen on bridal wear, sherwanis, clutches, and decorative panels.

Brassware – Moradabad’s Global Craft

Brassware:Moradabad, often dubbed the “Brass City” of India, is internationally acclaimed for its exquisite brassware. The city’s metal artisans craft intricately designed items ranging from traditional utensils and religious artifacts to modern decorative pieces like lamps, vases, and furniture. Combining engraving, embossing, and filigree work, Moradabad’s brassware is a major export product, contributing significantly to India's handicrafts economy.

Terracotta – The Rustic Charm of Gorakhpur

Terracotta : Gorakhpur is known for its distinctive terracotta craft, characterized by its reddish-brown clay sculptures and pottery. Artisans mold and carve clay into intricate forms such as animal figurines, temple décor, and traditional cookware. The craft has deep-rooted connections with rural and religious life and has recently gained a GI (Geographical Indication) tag, enhancing its visibility and authenticity. The vibrant horse and elephant figurines are especially popular.

Wood Carving – Saharanpur’s Intricate Legacy

Wood Carving :Saharanpur in western Uttar Pradesh is one of the leading centers of wood carving in India. The region is known for its beautifully carved furniture, screens, doors, and home décor items, often made from sheesham (Indian rosewood). Patterns typically include floral, geometric, and jali (lattice) designs. The craftsmanship here combines aesthetic elegance with functional design, making Saharanpur woodwork popular both in Indian markets and overseas

Carpet Weaving – Bhadohi and Mirzapur

The Carpet Belt :Bhadohi and Mirzapur form the heart of India’s carpet industry, often referred to as the "Carpet City" and the "Weaving Capital" respectively. The tradition of hand-knotted carpet weaving in these regions goes back over 500 years, influenced by Persian and Mughal techniques. Known for their high-quality wool and silk carpets with intricate floral and geometric patterns, these carpets are a major export item and have received GI tags as well. The industry also supports a vast number of artisans and weavers.

These crafts not only represent the artistic prowess of the people of Uttar Pradesh but also serve as a vital link to India's cultural and historical roots. They continue to adapt and evolve, blending tradition with modern design sensibilities to reach global markets.
